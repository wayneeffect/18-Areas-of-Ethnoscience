**Pharmacognosy**—the study of natural products (primarily from plants, but also microbes, marine organisms, and minerals) for medicinal use—is experiencing renewed momentum in 2026. It integrates advanced analytical techniques, molecular biology, nanotechnology, and data-driven approaches with traditional knowledge, supporting drug discovery, quality control, standardization, and sustainable development of herbal medicines.

### Key Trends and Advances (2025–2026)

- **Integration with Modern Drug Discovery**: Natural products (NPs) remain vital, with reviews highlighting their role in providing structural diversity and multi-target activity. A 2025 update emphasizes ongoing contributions to new therapeutics, while analyses of approvals (2014–2025) show steady NP-derived drugs and antibody-drug conjugates. Focus areas include bioassay-guided isolation and new pharmacophores.

- **Technological Innovations**:
  - Advanced analytical tools (LC-MS, GC-MS, NMR) for phytochemical profiling.
  - Molecular pharmacognosy: DNA barcoding for authentication, genomics/transcriptomics for biosynthesis pathways.
  - Nano-phytomedicine: Liposomes, phytosomes, and other nano-formulations to improve bioavailability and targeted delivery (e.g., enhanced curcumin applications).
  - In silico methods, AI, and computational approaches for screening and optimization.

- **Quality Control, Standardization, and Sustainability**: Emphasis on validation of herbal products, nutraceuticals, and safety. Research addresses antimicrobial applications (e.g., metal nanoparticles), antioxidant properties, and specific plants for conditions like sickle cell or inflammation.

- **Applications**: Marine pharmacognosy, medicinal plants for women’s health, anti-infectives, and bridging molecular to pre-clinical insights. Special issues explore these themes.

- Special issues in *Biomolecules* and reviews on advances in pharmacognosy for drug development.

The field bridges traditional knowledge with cutting-edge science, addressing challenges like biodiversity loss, standardization, and equitable benefit-sharing. It strongly overlaps with ethnomedicine, ethnobotany, and integrative health, contributing to pipelines in oncology, antimicrobials, and chronic disease management.
