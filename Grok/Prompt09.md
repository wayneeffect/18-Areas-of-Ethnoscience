**Ethnomicrobiology is an emerging interdisciplinary field** at the intersection of ethnobiology, microbiology, and cultural anthropology. It studies how human societies perceive, manage, interact with, and utilize microorganisms (bacteria, yeasts, molds, etc.) through traditional knowledge and practices. As of mid-2026, the field is gaining momentum, moving from descriptive studies (especially of fermentation) toward theoretical frameworks, biocultural conservation, and applications in food systems, health, agriculture, and sustainability.

### Key Milestone: Formalizing the Field
- A foundational 2024 review, *"The dawn of ethnomicrobiology: an interdisciplinary research field on interactions between humans and microorganisms"* by César Ojeda-Linares and colleagues (published in *Journal of Ethnobiology and Ethnomedicine*), proposed ethnomicrobiology as a distinct subfield within ethnobiology. It emphasizes human-microbe relationships beyond visible biota, covering fermentation, soil management, traditional medicine, composting, and more. It frames these as expressions of biocultural diversity and calls for integrating traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) with modern microbiology.

- Earlier precedents include JP Tamang’s work on “ethno-microbiology” of Indian fermented foods and beverages (focusing on indigenous knowledge of natural fermentation) and earlier studies on agricultural microbes like *Rhizobium*.

### Core Themes and Recent Activity
- **Fermentation and Food Systems**: Strong focus on traditional fermented foods/drinks (e.g., Mexican agave/maize/cactus beverages, Indian ethnic ferments, global examples). Research explores microbial consortia, organoleptic qualities, health benefits (probiotics, nutrition), and cultural practices. Exhibitions like “Entangled Cultures: How Humans and Microbes Co-create through Fermentation” (2025–2026) highlight public engagement.

- **Biocultural Conservation and Microbial Heritage**: Studies address conserving “microbial landscapes,” impacts of agricultural practices on beneficial microbes, and risks to traditional knowledge amid modernization and climate change. A 2026 *Journal of Ethnobiology* article discusses “Microbial Heritage at Risk.”

- **Broader Applications**: Soil health, traditional medicine, agriculture (e.g., nitrogen-fixing bacteria), and co-evolutionary human-microbe dynamics. It bridges senses (smell, taste, texture) with microbial ecology and promotes “microbial managers” in indigenous communities.

- **Interdisciplinary Growth**: Links to ethnobotany, medical anthropology, food microbiology, and sustainability. Podcasts, lectures (e.g., Ojeda-Linares in 2025–2026), and calls for more experimental/validation studies are increasing visibility.

### Conferences and Community
Ethnomicrobiology topics appear within broader **ethnobiology** gatherings:

The field remains small but dynamic, with potential for growth in planetary health, sustainable fermentation, and microbiome conservation. It complements related areas like ethnobotany and integrative health by highlighting the “invisible” microbial partners in human cultures.
