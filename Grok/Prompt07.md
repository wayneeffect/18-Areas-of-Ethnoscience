**Medical anthropology (or the cultural anthropology of medicine)** continues to evolve as a vibrant, critical field in 2026. It examines health, illness, care, bodies, and medical systems through cultural, political, and social lenses, with strong emphasis on power dynamics, inequalities, lived experiences, and intersections with global issues like authoritarianism, climate, technology, and equity.

### Key Recent Trends and Themes

- **Authoritarianism, Politics, and Critical Care**: Recent SMA (Society for Medical Anthropology) writings address the "specter of authoritarianism" and its impacts on health, care practices, and institutions. This includes analyses of political influences on medicine, borders, migration, and institutional trust.

- **Disability, Chronic Conditions, and Everyday Care**: Growing focus on disability anthropology (e.g., 2026 DRIG Essay Prize), intellectual disability, aging/dementia prevention affordances, and improvisational care practices. Research explores how people navigate expectations, stigma, and habitable worlds in contexts like India, Canada, and beyond.

- **Reproduction, HIV/STIs, and Infectious Disease Legacies**: Studies on reproduction politics, childbirth, and living with HIV amid overlapping pandemics (e.g., COVID-19 impacts on young women in Kenya). Broader work on war, disease, and colonial legacies persists.

- **Global Health, Equity, and Decolonial Approaches**: Emphasis on community praxis (e.g., Black Feminist Health Science Studies), transforming the field through collaboration, and critiquing neoliberal health models. Attention to traditional medicine, environmental health, and planetary dimensions.

- **Institutions, Trials, and Biopolitics**: Ethnographies of IRBs/clinical trials, prisons, borders, and psychopharmacy in contexts like empire or security.

### Publications and Journals (2025–2026)
- **Medical Anthropology Quarterly (MAQ)**: Volume 40, Issue 1 (March 2026) features articles on HIV stigma in Kenya, dementia prevention in Canada, and scientific rituals in Israeli clinical trials. Earlier 2025 issues cover diverse topics with open-access options.
- **Medical Anthropology** (Taylor & Francis): Ongoing articles into mid-2026 on varied ethnographic topics.
- New and forthcoming books address illness/enlightenment, autism/advocacy, psychopharmacy, prison care, and more (from presses like UC Press, Berghahn, Rutgers).

The field remains committed to ethnographic depth while engaging urgent contemporary crises, interdisciplinary collaboration (e.g., with public health, disability studies, STS), and reflexive, activist-oriented approaches. It balances documentation of suffering/inequality with explorations of agency, resistance, and alternative futures.
