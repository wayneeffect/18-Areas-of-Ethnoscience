**As of mid-2026**, developments in federal, Michigan, and tribal law reflect the second Trump administration’s deregulatory push, state-level labor and consumer reforms in Michigan, and ongoing tribal sovereignty issues. Here’s a high-level overview of notable recent changes.

### Federal Law (119th Congress & Trump Administration)
The 119th Congress (2025–2026) and executive actions emphasize immigration enforcement, deregulation (especially AI and DEI), federal workforce changes, and targeted spending.

- **Major Legislation**: The *Laken Riley Act* (Jan. 2025) mandates detention for certain non-citizens arrested for theft-related crimes and allows states to sue over immigration enforcement. The *One Big Beautiful Bill Act* (July 2025) includes significant Medicaid cuts, new immigration fees, tax changes (e.g., QSBS benefits), and healthcare reforms. The NDAA for 2026 authorizes ~$901 billion in defense spending. Other enactments include the Cape Fox Land Entitlement Finalization Act and various public laws on retirement, medals, and infrastructure.

- **Executive & Regulatory Actions**: Early 2025 actions revoked Biden-era AI safety rules and issued new EOs promoting U.S. AI leadership while seeking to preempt inconsistent state laws. DEI-related orders target federal contractors. Immigration expansions (travel bans, detention funding) and federal funding pauses/rescissions occurred. Student loan and Pell Grant changes are rolling out.

- **Indian/Tribal Law**: Activity includes the Tribal Labor Sovereignty Act proposals and land/homeownership bills. The Tribal Supreme Court Project marks its 25th anniversary amid ongoing cases on sovereignty, voting, and treaty rights.

- **Employment & Other**: NLRB and DOL rules on joint employers and independent contractors shifted. EEOC changes and federal grant certifications (e.g., on DEI/immigration) emerged.

### Michigan State Law
Michigan’s 103rd Legislature (under Gov. Gretchen Whitmer) passed a relatively low volume of bills in 2025, with many taking effect in late 2025 or 2026. Focus areas include labor/wages, consumer protections, cannabis, and targeted memorials.

- **Labor & Wages (Key 2026 Changes)**: Minimum wage rose to **$13.73/hour** (Jan. 1, 2026), heading to $15 by 2027 under amendments to the Improved Workforce Opportunity Wage Act (Senate Bill 8, effective Feb. 2025). Tipped wage rules adjusted (tip credit phased). Maximum weekly unemployment benefits increased (to ~$530). Earned Sick Time Act expansions and youth employment hour restrictions for ages 14–15 apply. Temporary state income tax exemption on tips and overtime (through 2028).

- **Other Notable Laws**: 24% wholesale excise tax on recreational marijuana transfers (Jan. 2026, on top of existing taxes). Consumer protections, housing, education, and anti-discrimination measures (e.g., insurance fines). A Civil War-era Native American veterans memorial highway designation.

- **Courts**: Michigan Supreme Court and Court of Appeals rulings on contract limitations periods, treaty rights (e.g., non-federally recognized groups), and tribal sovereign immunity for entities.

### Tribal Law (Focus on Michigan’s 12 Federally Recognized Tribes)
Tribal law remains sovereign and distinct, with federal Indian law providing the overarching framework. Michigan tribes (e.g., Sault Ste. Marie Tribe of Chippewa Indians, Saginaw Chippewa, Little Traverse Bay Bands) handle internal governance, courts, gaming, and services.

- **Internal Governance**: Tribes like the Sault Tribe advanced constitutional amendments (e.g., three-branch separation) via secretarial elections in 2026. Court dockets and community updates (e.g., Saginaw Chippewa) continue routinely.

- **State-Tribal Interactions**: Michigan courts address treaty rights (e.g., fishing cases involving Mackinac Bands), sovereign immunity limits for tribal entities, and the Michigan Indian Family Preservation Act (MIFPA) applicability. A new handbook introduces tribal law to Michigan law students.

- **Federal Overlaps**: Tribes navigate federal policy shifts (e.g., labor sovereignty bills, land entitlements, consultation requirements). Co-management, gaming compacts, and resource rights remain active.

**Overall Context**: Federal policy is in flux with rapid EOs and deregulation. Michigan prioritizes worker protections and cost-of-living measures. Tribal law emphasizes sovereignty preservation amid federal changes and state collaborations.
