**Pharmacology in 2026 is marked by accelerated integration of AI and computational modeling, maturation of precision and model-informed drug development (MIDD), next-generation modalities (e.g., oral GLP-1s, targeted protein degraders), and a strong pipeline of novel approvals building on 2025’s robust output.** The field emphasizes faster translation from discovery to clinic, improved safety/efficacy prediction, and addressing major therapeutic areas like obesity, oncology, rare diseases, and immunology.

### Key Trends and Advances

- **AI, Computational Innovation, and Model-Informed Drug Development (MIDD)**: AI has shifted from screening to generative design, shortening discovery timelines significantly (e.g., 40–50% reductions reported). Mechanistic modeling and MIDD are now mainstream for optimizing dosing, predicting responses, and improving efficiency. Multimodal data, physics-informed AI, and in silico tools enhance target identification, toxicity prediction, and personalized regimens.

- **Obesity, Diabetes, and Metabolic Therapies**: GLP-1 and multi-agonist drugs dominate. Highlights include Eli Lilly’s oral orforglipron (potential approval ~March 2026, strong weight loss with pill convenience) and retatrutide (triple agonist showing up to ~28% weight loss in late-stage trials, plus broad cardiometabolic benefits). Next-gen injectables and orals are expanding access and efficacy.

- **Novel Drug Approvals and Pipeline**: 
  - **2025 recap**: FDA approved ~45–55 novel drugs (plus cell/gene therapies), with ~43–79 novel active substances globally depending on definitions. Strong first-in-class entries in oncology, rare diseases, and precision medicine.
  - **2026 outlook**: Continued momentum with drugs like targeted protein degraders (oncology), new women’s health options, rare disease gene therapies, and innovations in allergy (e.g., Anaphylm), hypertension, and more. Late-stage readouts and approvals expected in oncology, immunology, and neurology.

- **Precision Medicine and New Modalities**: Advances in pharmacogenomics, biomarkers, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), in vivo gene editing (e.g., CRISPR), and personalized dosing. Expanded indications (e.g., teplizumab for type 1 diabetes delay) and better delivery systems (nanoparticles, oral formulations) improve outcomes.

- **Safety, Toxicology, and Regulatory Shifts**: Greater use of human-relevant models (organoids, chips), AI for toxicity, and focus on excipient hypersensitivity, drug interactions, and real-world evidence. Regulatory emphasis on efficiency while maintaining rigor.
