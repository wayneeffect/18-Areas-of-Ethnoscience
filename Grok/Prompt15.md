**Ethnoforestry** (or ethnobotany-focused forestry) integrates traditional ecological knowledge (TEK), Indigenous and local practices, and cultural perspectives into forest management, conservation, and sustainable use. It emphasizes people-centered approaches that combine customary ways of creating, conserving, managing, and utilizing forest resources with modern scientific methods.

As of mid-2026, the field gains visibility through Indigenous co-management, restoration projects, climate resilience, and interdisciplinary links to ethnobiology and sustainable development. It addresses biodiversity loss, livelihood security, and equity in forest governance.

### Key Trends and Developments

- **Indigenous Knowledge Integration and Co-Management**: Growing emphasis on returning lands to tribes and incorporating practices like cultural burning, sacred groves, and traditional fallow management. Examples include California proposals for tribal oversight of ancestral redwoods and Vancouver Island First Nations gaining control in Clayoquot Sound for ecosystem-based models.

- **Field Trials and Applied Research**: The Olympic Natural Resources Center (Washington, USA) ethnoforestry field trials (ongoing since ~2021) test people-focused management for early seral habitats, understory plants valued by communities/wildlife, and timber production. Treatments combine planted understory species with Douglas-fir/red alder, browse controls, and agroforestry-style planting.

- **Restoration, Climate, and Livelihoods**: Links to global restoration efforts (e.g., International Day of Forests 2026 themes) and policies integrating TEK for resilience. Horizon scans highlight forces like AI, mineral extraction, and Indigenous rights in forest futures.

- **Academic and Policy Momentum**: Special issues and calls explore ethnoforestry’s links to socio-ecological changes, biodiversity, food security, and human health. Traditional forest knowledge is framed as actionable science, not just folklore.

- **Society of Ethnobiology Annual Conference**: May 20–23, Sewanee, Tennessee — includes ethics, collaborations, and forest-related sessions.
- **Society for Ethnobotany Congress**: May 31–June 4, Montpellier, France (“Undisciplined Ethnobotany”) — covers collective stewardship and planetary health.

Ethnoforestry builds on earlier foundational work (e.g., Deep Narayan Pandey’s contributions from India) while expanding globally through participatory, rights-based, and climate-adaptive approaches. It overlaps strongly with ethnobotany, ethnomedicine, and integrative forest management.
