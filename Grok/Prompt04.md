**Ethnobotany—the study of relationships between people and plants**—is thriving in 2026 with stronger emphasis on quantitative methods, climate adaptation, biocultural conservation, digital tools, and decolonial/undisciplined approaches that bridge traditional knowledge with modern challenges.

### Major Trends and Innovations

- **Quantitative and Data-Driven Ethnobotany**: There's growing integration of statistics, machine learning, bibliometrics, and indices (e.g., Spatial Availability Score, Botanical Ethnoknowledge Index). A 2026 bibliometric analysis reviewed statistical, mathematical, and data science approaches from 2016–2025. Studies increasingly use quantitative ethnobotanical indices alongside traditional surveys, and machine learning for modeling uses/satisfaction.

- **Climate Change, Conservation, and Resilience**: Ethnobotanical knowledge supports adaptation, ecosystem restoration, and documenting climate impacts on culturally important species. Projects like the Shinnecock Ethnobotany Project combine traditional knowledge with climate indicators. Research highlights plants' roles in biodiversity conservation, food security, and addressing the triple crisis (biodiversity, poverty, climate).

- **Medicinal Plants and Applied Research**: Ongoing global documentation of traditional uses for ailments (e.g., inflammation, mental health, reproductive issues, wounds, rheumatic diseases). Studies span diverse regions (Pakistan, Algeria, Mexico, India, Thailand, etc.), with focus on validation, phytochemistry, and sustainable use. Special issues explore advances in ethnobotany and phytochemistry.

- **Digital and Technological Integration**: Digitization of traditional knowledge, databases, GIS, DNA barcoding, AI for identification/analysis, and ethical data practices. This aids preservation while enabling broader collaboration, though challenges around intellectual property remain.

- **Biocultural Diversity, Indigenous Knowledge, and Education**: Emphasis on preserving endangered knowledge, community-led research, and integrating ethnobotany into education and sustainable development. Applied/non-academic practitioners are gaining visibility.

### Key 2026 Highlights
- **Society for Ethnobotany (SEB) Congress**: "Undisciplined Ethnobotany" in Montpellier, France (May 31–June 4, 2026). It rethinks boundaries as a relational practice across species, epistemologies, and disciplines, focusing on Indigenous rights, agroecology, planetary health, and collective stewardship.
- **Publications**: New issues of *Ethnobotany Research and Applications* (Vol. 34, 2026), *Economic Botany*, *Plants and People* newsletter, and special issues on medicinal plants and conservation.
- **Society of Ethnobiology Conference**: May 20–23, 2026, in Sewanee, Tennessee, with themes including climate, fire ecology, and applied ethnobiology.
- Awards and recognitions for books on Indian ethnobotany and related topics.

The field balances documentation of rapidly disappearing knowledge with actionable contributions to sustainability, health, and cultural resilience. It increasingly collaborates across disciplines (e.g., botany education, anthropology, pharmacology) while centering ethical, participatory, and Indigenous-led approaches.
